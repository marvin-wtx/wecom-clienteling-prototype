# Creative Divergence System

Use this for open-generative or evidence-derived branded prototypes when the user wants strong variety across brands. It gives high-level agents room to create better differences without weakening evidence, shell, page-depth, or workbench constraints.

## Purpose

V2.7 prevents weak generic output. This layer helps strong agents produce richer variety by changing the product concept, information architecture, and interaction model, not just styling.

The goal is **coherent divergence**:

- different brands may choose different business axes;
- different pages may use different module grammars;
- different cases should remain distinct after removing palette, typography, logo, and product imagery;
- every creative move must improve demo clarity, workbench usability, brand fit, or clienteling credibility.

## Creative Thesis

Before designing, write one sentence:

`This prototype treats the brand's clienteling workbench as <operating metaphor> because <evidence or business rationale>.`

Good operating metaphors are business mechanisms, not moods:

- service concierge;
- appointment atelier;
- replenishment radar;
- product-interest studio;
- relationship repair desk;
- event attendance cockpit;
- manager exception review;
- content-to-conversation engine;
- loyalty moment planner;
- post-purchase care timeline.

Do not use vague style theses such as premium, modern, elegant, Gen Z, minimalist, or luxury.

## Inspiration Transposition

When using external UI references, extract mechanisms rather than copying looks:

1. Reference pattern: task list, schedule board, inbox triage, account dossier, checkout review, project timeline, CRM pipeline, support queue, or dashboard drilldown.
2. Extracted mechanism: grouping, progression, decision point, empty state, filtering model, quick action, status display, or recovery path.
3. Clienteling translation: how the mechanism helps customer operation, task execution, appointment prep, content sharing, native WeCom handoff, or manager review.
4. Rejection boundary: what not to copy because it would hurt WeCom shell, brand evidence, or operational scan speed.

Never cite generic inspiration as proof of the target brand's internal workflow.

## Divergence Levers

Choose at least four high-impact levers for open-generative cases:

- `businessAxis`: queue, service, appointment, content, product interest, replenishment, loyalty, event, exception, relationship, or lifecycle.
- `navigationModel`: item count, order, center action, role variation, object-first vs journey-first, or hub-and-action.
- `homeNarrative`: triage, agenda, radar, cockpit, dossier, calendar, studio, exception review, pulse, or command center.
- `detailArchitecture`: tabbed dossier, timeline, action-first sheet, canvas, checklist, split preparation, resource view, or outcome capture.
- `taskModel`: checklist, target-progress worklist, conversation-led action, content-preview workflow, appointment conversion, replenishment loop, or manager escalation.
- `dataStory`: customer value, lifecycle, service readiness, product interest, event participation, content engagement, appointment conversion, task compliance, or team exception.
- `signatureInteraction`: create, route, confirm, hand off, capture result, write back, recover blocked state, or compare alternatives.
- `visualAnchor`: typography, product object, material detail, spatial rhythm, controlled color field, data display, editorial image, or precise grid.

Palette, font, border radius, and brand copy are low-impact levers by themselves.

## Portfolio Contrast

When multiple cases exist, define the target case's contrast against at least one prior or likely case:

- different business axis;
- different navigation logic;
- different home narrative;
- different detail architecture;
- different signature interaction;
- different density or visual anchor.

If the contrast is only "different brand color" or "different campaign story", it is not enough.

## Coherence Proof

Every creative direction must prove coherence:

- The navigation follows the business axis.
- The home first viewport supports the selected operating metaphor.
- At least two detail pages use architectures that match their job.
- The signature interaction changes state, route, result, or handoff.
- Workbench usability remains faster than brand decoration.
- Evidence-backed terms stay separate from assumptions.

If a creative move cannot be explained through this chain, remove it.

## High-Level Agent Freedom

Strong agents may:

- create unusual but useful combinations, such as appointment-led navigation with content-to-conversation task execution;
- vary tabbar order, item count, and center-action policy;
- make one brand's home an agenda and another brand's home a relationship pulse;
- use progressive disclosure, timelines, matrices, dossiers, canvases, and ledgers where they fit the work;
- use restrained or expressive brand moments if they clarify the demo path.

Strong agents may not:

- ignore source authority;
- invent proprietary brand operations;
- weaken page information depth;
- break protected WeCom shell/native replicas;
- pass off a decorative hero as a workbench.
